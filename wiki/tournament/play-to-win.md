# Play to Win, Deals, and the Two-Player Exception

The VEKN tournament rules treat **play-to-win** as a sportsmanship requirement, **acknowledge but don't enforce** table deals, and impose a special **two-player exception** that voids prior deals once only two Methuselahs remain. The rules also forbid **infinite loops** that exploit the time limit [src-032 §4.8, §4.9, §3.5, §3.6].

## Play to Win — definition [src-032 §4.8]

> Players must not play toward goals that conflict with the goal of the game as stated in the V:TES rulebook (e.g., attacking certain players on the basis of their VEKN ratings or overall tournament standing, etc.).

In a tournament, play-to-win means:

1. Play to **earn a Game Win** if it is reasonably possible.
2. If a Game Win is not reasonably possible, play to earn **as many Victory Points as possible**.

Examples of conduct that violate play-to-win:
- Letting a friend's prey survive because their tournament standing benefits more from a draw.
- Choosing to die quickly so that a specific predator advances over another player they dislike.
- Playing to "deny" a Game Win to a leader rather than to maximise your own VP yield.

Violations are reviewed under the VEKN Penalty Guidelines and may include disqualification.

## In the final round [src-032 §4.8 / §3.7.5]

Inside the final, *playing to win* means **playing to finish as the tournament winner** (the finalist with the most VP scored in the final only — see [[scoring#The final round src-032 §3.1.3 / §3.7.5]]). Playing for second is a play-to-win violation in the final.

## Deals — acknowledged, not enforced [src-032 §4.8]

The basic game rules don't enforce deals, and neither do the tournament rules. The tournament rules **do** acknowledge that:

> A deal which represents the best interests of the players involved at the time the deal is made is allowed to be honored, even when the normal play-to-win rule would indicate that a deal should be broken.

This carve-out applies only when **making the deal was itself play-to-win** at the time. It does not give cover to deals that were never in the dealmaker's interest.

> It is also allowable to break such a deal, of course.

Bottom line: deals are a social contract, not a rules contract. A judge will not punish you for breaking one, *but* the deal must have been play-to-win when made — otherwise both making it and honouring it are violations.

## Two-Player Exception [src-032 §4.8]

> When only two Methuselahs remain, the tournament rules no longer acknowledge any deals. Prior deals are voided, even if they were play-to-win when made. When only two Methuselahs remain, both Methuselahs must play to win based only on game state, without regard to any deals.

This is the single most important rule to internalise for any close late game — once the table is down to two, every prior promise loses its rules backing. Continuing to honour it because "we agreed" is not a defence; the judge expects both players to **play the game state**.

For our local playgroup this is a frequent source of confusion: a survival-pact across rounds can hold during five-handed play, but once a table degenerates to a 1-on-1 it dissolves automatically. See [[../playgroup/common-mistakes]] if a real incident gets logged.

## Conceding a game [src-032 §3.5]

> Players may concede a game at any time provided all but one of the players agree to concede, and provided it doesn't violate the play to win rule, with the result that the game is recorded as if the remaining player had succeeded in ousting the conceding players in sequence.

Two requirements stack:

1. **Unanimous consent** of every player except the one who's left standing.
2. **Compatible with play-to-win** — the players can't concede simply to deliver a Game Win to a tournament-standing favourite.

> Players who attempt to bribe, coerce, or otherwise improperly induce their opponents to concede will be subject to the appropriate section of the V:EKN Penalty Guidelines.

Cash, booster packs, future-round favours, threats — all out.

## Intentional draw [src-032 §3.6]

Players may mutually agree to an **intentional draw** at any time before the game results are submitted. If the offer is declined, the game continues with no further coercion. The result is recorded as if the round had hit time:

- All surviving Methuselahs receive **0.5 VP** for survival.
- The sole survivor (if any) receives a full **1 VP** instead.
- See [[scoring]] for how that translates to TP and GW.

## Infinite loops [src-032 §4.9]

> When a loop is detected (meaning game state has been completely restored to a previous state and could be repeated without limit except for the round's time limit), the activity cannot be begun again (until and unless game state changes). This includes actions, sequences of combats, rounds of combat, and everything else.

**Game state** for loop-detection purposes includes:

- Every player's hand, ash heap, and library.
- Available maneuvers and presses from cards in play.
- Master out-of-turn plays still available.

Practical implication — once you've been around a loop once and the game state is identical to what it was *before* the loop, you can't run the loop a second time. This blocks "infinite presses with Carlton until time" or "permanent Conditioning chain" tricks that deliberately stall the round.

Note that **breaking the loop is sufficient** — if anything has shifted (a cantrip gained an Edge, a card was discarded, a master out-of-turn was used, etc.) the next iteration is legal because the state has changed.
